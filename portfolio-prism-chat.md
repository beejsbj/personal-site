The companions are doing real work and I don't think you've noticed yet. Otto, Rose, Claw — these aren't mascots, they're your actual values externalized as findable agents. Curiosity, loving-kindness, courage/loyalty. The user gains capacities by *finding* embodiments of those qualities in a falling stream of symbols. That's a small piece of cosmology, not a gimmick. It also means the abilities can't be arbitrary game-feel — they have to feel like what those qualities actually do in the world. That's the constraint that makes the design problem interesting instead of decorative.

So rather than reaching for ability ideas cold, work backwards from the verb each quality performs:

Otto / playful curiosity — the verb is *probing*. Reaching into things to see what they are. So left-click could reveal: hover-poke a falling symbol and it unfolds into what it actually is (a function, a memory, a half-finished project, a link). Curiosity makes the opaque legible. It doesn't grab or consume; it touches and learns.

Rose / honest loving-kindness — the verb is *seeing-clearly-with-warmth*. Not softening, not flattering. Right-click could be something like: hold over any element and it shows you its truth — what it's really for, what it costs, what it's hiding. The honest version. Crystalline because it cuts; organic because it doesn't wound. That maps to what you've actually said Rose is for.

Claw / courage and loyalty — the verb is *staying*. Holding ground. Middle-click could anchor — pin a thing so the simulation can't sweep it away, or stand between the user and something the environment is doing to them. Loyalty as the refusal to let something be lost in the churn.

You'll notice none of those are combat abilities. That matters. The Matrix-genre default for "powers" is fighting, and your values aren't martial. If the abilities feel like spells in a game, the whole piece tips into pastiche. If they feel like *ways of relating*, the piece stays yours.

It's the only element so far that gestures at *another presence* in the space — not the user, not a companion you've gained, something else. The answer shapes the tone of the whole interior.

And — the orbit mechanic for Otto and Rose. Once gained, they orbit the cursor. That means at the end of the piece, after all the cube-traversal and the smudging and the wake-up, the user is moving through this space with your values literally circling them. That's a quiet, almost devotional image. Worth knowing you've built that in.

Quick gut-check on the gamey abstractions then, since that's where you landed:

Left-click / Otto / probing — feels right as something tactile and a little chaotic. Symbols react when poked. Maybe a small ink-splash or a ripple. The *find* verb.

Right-click / Rose / soothing — wants something slower than left-click. A hold, maybe, or a small bloom that grows while held. Mechanically different feel from the probe so the two abilities don't blur into "click on stuff."

Middle-click / Claw / pin — works as a brief anchor. Useful in the Matrix scene specifically because everything's *falling* — pinning fights the medium. That's a nice small piece of game-feel that doesn't require justification.

One practical note since you linked the HIC origin trial: HTML-in-canvas is still experimental and gated, so the inside-the-cube HTML faces will work great in Chrome with the flag and degrade everywhere else. The three.js CSS3DRenderer approach (the texture_html example you linked is adjacent) is the safer fallback — it composites real DOM over WebGL rather than rendering DOM into a texture, which means no prism/filter shaders touching the HTML directly, but the traversal and theme-swaps still work. Worth deciding early which one you're targeting because it shapes what's possible for the between-faces effects. The smudging and burn-in are pure shader work on the WebGL side either way, so those are safe.
